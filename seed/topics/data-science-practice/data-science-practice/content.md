This family is the judgment the math doesn't capture — the skills for taking statistics out into real, messy data without fooling yourself. It's the smallest cluster on purpose: most of what makes a good practitioner is *how* they apply the other families, and that shows up here as habits rather than theorems.

The cluster behind these words is the practice arm of the graph.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: exploratory-data-analysis, anchor: fam-dsp-eda -->

## Exploratory data analysis

Look before you model. Summaries, distributions, and the handful of plots that catch the broken column, the impossible value, and the skew that will wreck a naive average. The cheapest mistakes to fix are the ones you find here, first.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: ab-testing, anchor: fam-dsp-ab -->

## A/B testing

Randomized experiments in the wild: design them so the comparison is fair, then read the result without talking yourself into an effect that isn't there. This is hypothesis testing with all the real-world ways to get it wrong attached.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: cross-validation, anchor: fam-dsp-cv -->

## Cross-validation

Estimating how a model performs on data it *hasn't* seen, instead of trusting the fit you can see. The fit on your training data is almost always optimistic; cross-validation is how you get an honest number.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: bias-variance-tradeoff, anchor: fam-dsp-bv -->

## The bias–variance tradeoff

The central tension of modeling: too simple and the model underfits (high bias); too flexible and it chases noise (high variance). Most practical modeling decisions are a move along this one axis.

Begin at [exploratory data analysis](/topic/exploratory-data-analysis) — it's the habit everything else rests on. The rest of this family is newer and still filling in; it leans on [hypothesis testing](/topic/hypothesis-testing) and [regression](/topic/simple-linear-regression), so those are good detours first.
