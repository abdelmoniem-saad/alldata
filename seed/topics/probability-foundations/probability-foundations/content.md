Probability is the language the rest of statistics is written in. Before you can estimate a parameter, test a claim, or fit a model, you need a precise way to talk about uncertainty. This family builds that language from the ground up — and it ends with the two limit theorems that the whole field leans on.

The cluster behind these words is the probability arm of the graph. Scroll, and it'll walk you through it one idea at a time.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: sample-spaces, anchor: fam-prob-sample-spaces -->

## Sample spaces and events

Everything starts here: the set of outcomes that *could* happen, and the events you actually care about inside it. Get the sample space right and the rest is arithmetic; get it wrong and every number after it is wrong too.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: conditional-probability, anchor: fam-prob-conditional -->

## Conditional probability

How a probability changes once you know something else happened. This is the engine room — independence, Bayes' theorem, and most of the paradoxes that make probability feel slippery all come straight out of conditioning.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: bayes-theorem, anchor: fam-prob-bayes -->

## Bayes' theorem

Reversing the conditional: going from "probability of the evidence given the cause" to "probability of the cause given the evidence." It's the rule behind every diagnostic-test surprise — why a positive result on an accurate test can still mean you're probably fine.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: expectation, anchor: fam-prob-expectation -->

## Expectation and variance

The expected value is the long-run average — the single number you'd bet on. Variance, its companion, measures how far reality typically strays from that average. Together they summarize a random quantity in two numbers.

---

<!-- block: graph_view, target: central-limit-theorem, anchor: fam-prob-clt -->

## The central limit theorem

Why the bell curve is everywhere: average enough independent things and the result is normal — almost regardless of what you started with. It's the reason so much of statistics can assume normality, and the bridge from this family into inference.
